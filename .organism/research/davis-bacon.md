## 1. DAVIS-BACON ACT FUNDAMENTALS

### What Is the Davis-Bacon Act?

Enacted on March 3, 1931 (40 U.S.C. §§ 3141–3148), the Davis-Bacon Act (DBA) requires that contractors and subcontractors on **federally funded or federally assisted construction contracts** pay their laborers and mechanics **no less than the locally prevailing wages and fringe benefits** for corresponding work on similar projects in the area.

The Act was passed to prevent the federal government from using outside (non-local) labor to undercut local wage standards, particularly during Depression-era public works projects.

**Statutory reference:** 40 U.S.C. § 3141 *et seq.*

---

### Dollar Threshold and Triggering Conditions

| Trigger | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Federal DBA threshold | **$2,000** (contract value for construction, alteration, or repair) |
| Contract Work Hours & Safety Standards Act (CWHSSA) | **$100,000** (overtime protections kick in) |

The $2,000 threshold is so low that virtually every federal construction contract is covered. It applies to:

- **Construction, alteration, or repair** (including painting and decorating)
- **Public buildings or public works**
- Contracts to which **the United States or the District of Columbia is a party**

**Key: The DBA applies both directly (federal party to the contract) and indirectly through ~71 "Related Acts"** — federal statutes that extend Davis-Bacon standards to projects receiving federal grants, loans, loan guarantees, or insurance funding. The total scope of DBA + Related Acts applies to an estimated **$217 billion in annual federal and federally assisted construction spending** (about 63% of all government construction).

---

### Which Projects Require It?

**Direct Davis-Bacon coverage:**
- USACE (Army Corps of Engineers) construction contracts
- GSA building construction
- VA hospital and facility projects
- Direct federal agency construction contracts in any amount over $2,000

**Davis-Bacon Related Acts (DBRA) coverage — federally assisted projects:**
- **HUD:** Low-income housing, CDBG-funded projects, HOME program
- **DOT/FHWA:** All federal-aid highway projects (23 U.S.C. § 113)
- **EPA:** Water and sewer treatment works (Clean Water Act, 33 U.S.C. § 1372)
- **FEMA:** Public Assistance and BRIC programs (notably, NOT Hazard Mitigation Assistance)
- **DOE:** Energy infrastructure and weatherization
- **SBA:** Disaster loan-funded construction
- **USDA:** Rural development and water projects

**Private projects that may trigger Davis-Bacon:**
- Projects receiving federal tax credits (e.g., Low-Income Housing Tax Credit)
- Projects with federal loan guarantees
- Some TIF (Tax Increment Financing) structures where federal funds flow through

---

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Penalty Type | Amount/Duration |
|---|---|
| Back wages owed | No cap — full underpayment to all affected workers |
| Civil monetary penalties (per violation) | Up to **$13,508** per violation (as of 2025, inflation-adjusted) |
| Liquidated damages | Up to an amount equal to unpaid wages (can double liability) |
| Contract fund withholding | Agency can withhold payments until resolved |
| Debarment | Up to **3 years** from all federal contracting |
| Contract termination | For severe or repeated violations |
| Criminal penalties (falsified payrolls) | Under 18 U.S.C. § 1001: fine + up to **5 years imprisonment** |

**Post-2023 enforcement enhancements:**
- DOL can now **cross-withhold** back wages from a contractor's other federal contracts, even contracts with different agencies
- Prime contractors are now responsible for subcontractor back wages **regardless of intent**
- The 2023 Final Rule strengthened enforcement mechanisms significantly

**Real-world penalty examples (from enforcement data):**
- Mid-sized contractor: $180,000 on a single federal highway project
- Large GC: $2.8 million to resolve violations on multiple highway projects (400+ workers affected)
- Combined federal + state penalties on jointly funded projects: up to $85,000+ per violation incident

**What triggers investigation** (see Section 4 for detail):
1. Worker complaints (most common trigger)
2. Certified payroll discrepancies or missing WH-347 forms
3. DOL directed investigations in high-risk industries (construction is high-risk)
4. Referrals from other agencies (OSHA, IRS, state labor departments)
5. Media reports or public information

### Companion Laws to Davis-Bacon

| Law | Requirement |
|---|---|

## 2. WH-347 FORM — COMPLETE SPECIFICATION

### What Is WH-347?

Form WH-347 (OMB Control No. 1235-0008, Expires 01/31/2028) is the U.S. Department of Labor's official **weekly certified payroll form** for Davis-Bacon and Related Acts projects.

**Key facts:**
- Use of WH-347 is **optional** (contractors may use any form with equivalent content)
- Submission of certified payroll data **weekly** is **mandatory** under the Copeland Act (40 U.S.C. § 3145) and 29 CFR 5.5(a)(3)(ii)
- Filed with: the contracting agency (if federal party to contract) OR the applicant/sponsor/owner (on federally-assisted contracts)
- DOL now provides an **online fillable version** and an **annotated guide** (announced December 16, 2025)
- The DOL estimates **55 minutes** average to complete the form

**Where to find it:**  
[https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/forms/wh347](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/forms/wh347)  
Online fillable form: [https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/forms/wh347-web](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/forms/wh347-web)

### PAGE 1: Payroll Report — Complete Field List

#### HEADER SECTION

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| **Final DBRA Certified Payroll Checkbox** | Check to indicate this is the final week of work on the project for this entity |
| **Prime Contractor / Subcontractor Checkbox** | Check appropriate box to indicate role |
| **Project Name** | Name of the project being reported |
| **Project No. or Contract No.** | Project/contract number assigned by contracting agency (if available) |

| **Wage Determination No.** | All wage determination number(s) AND revision number(s) applicable to work performed in this pay period |
| **Week Ending Date** | Last day of the workweek for this pay period (workweek need not be Mon–Sun) |
| **Prime Contractor's/Subcontractor's Business Address** | Company's full business address |

#### WORKER COLUMNS (Columns 1A–9)

| Column | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1A** | Worker Entry No. | Sequential number starting at 1 for each worker row. If a worker performed multiple classifications, same entry number used on all rows for that worker |
| **1B** | Worker Last Name | Self-explanatory |
| **1C** | Worker First Name | Self-explanatory |
| **1D** | Worker Middle Initial | Self-explanatory |
| **1E** | Worker Identifying No. | Last 4 digits of SSN or any unique identifying number. **Full SSN must NOT be included.** |
| **2** | Journeyworker / Registered Apprentice | "J" for journeyworker; "RA" for registered apprentice (must include apprentice progression level) |
| **3** | Labor Classification | Work classification as listed in applicable wage determination(s). Must reflect **actual work performed**, not job title. If worker performed multiple classifications, separate row for each |
| **4** | Hours Worked Each Day | Header row: first letter of each day of workweek + corresponding date. Worker rows: Straight Time (ST) and Overtime (OT) hours per day |
| **5** | Total Hours Worked for Week | Sum of all hours in Column 4 |
| **6A** | Hourly Wage Rate Paid for ST and OT | Actual hourly rate paid for straight time (top) and overtime (bottom). Do NOT include cash fringe payments here |
| **6B** | Total Fringe Benefit Credit | Total credit claimed for bona fide fringe benefit plans (hours × hourly fringe credit from Page 2 Hourly Credit table) |
| **6C** | Payment in Lieu of Fringe Benefits | Total cash paid in lieu of fringe benefits (hours × cash-in-lieu hourly rate) |
| **7A** | Gross Amount Earned (this project) | Gross amount earned specifically on this covered project this week |
| **7B** | Gross Amount Earned for All Work | Total gross earned across ALL projects and work this week (including non-DBRA work) |
| **8** | Deductions for All Work | Broken into: Tax Withholdings / FICA / Other / Total Deductions. "Other" requires description in Additional Remarks if single deduction; addendum required if multiple deductions. All deductions must comply with 29 CFR Part 3 (Copeland Act) |
| **9** | Net Payment to Worker for All Work | Actual dollar paid to worker across all projects this week |

**CRITICAL CLASSIFICATION RULE:** If a worker performed multiple classifications in a week, the contractor MUST either (a) maintain an accurate breakdown of hours by classification and show separate rows, OR (b) if no breakdown was maintained, pay ALL hours at the HIGHEST applicable prevailing wage rate.

### Signature and Legal Status

- The Statement of Compliance is **NOT required to be notarized**
- However, it is **subject to 18 U.S.C. § 1001** — fine and/or up to **5 years imprisonment** for false statements
- **Legally valid electronic signatures are acceptable** — must be an electronic process that indicates acceptance and includes an electronic method of verifying the signer's identity
- **Photocopies and scanned copies of handwritten signatures do NOT satisfy the requirement**
- The person signing must have **actual knowledge** of the facts represented

---

### Submission Requirements

- **Frequency:** Weekly — for every week in which DBRA-covered work is performed
- **Submit to:** Contracting agency if federal party to contract; OR the applicant/sponsor/owner on federally-assisted contracts (who then transmits to the federal agency)
- **Prime contractor responsibility:** The GC is responsible for the submission of ALL certified payrolls by ALL subcontractors (29 CFR 5.5(a)(3)(ii)(A))
- **Electronic submission:** Contracting agencies or prime contractors may permit or require electronic submission systems — systems must support legally valid electronic signatures and provide access to records for the full 3-year retention period

---

### Common Errors That Cause Rejection

1. **Worker misclassification** — using the wrong trade classification or the contractor's internal job title instead of the wage determination classification
2. **Incomplete fringe benefit documentation** — failing to complete the Hourly Credit table when claiming plan credits; not accounting for all hours when computing credits
3. **Missing or incorrect wage determination number** — wrong WD number or revision number
4. **Failure to show multi-classification splits** — worker did two trades in one week but only one row submitted, and paid at the lower rate
5. **Full SSN included** — only last 4 digits are permitted
6. **Scanned/photocopied signature** — invalid; must be original electronic signature or wet ink
7. **Incorrect payroll number sequence** — gaps in sequential numbering, or restarting the sequence
8. **Deductions not itemized** — "Other" deductions requiring addendum not provided
9. **Gross amount only from this project, not all work** — Column 7B requires all-work gross
10. **Apprentice rate used without Box 4 certification** — must confirm DOL/SAA registration
11. **Cash-in-lieu not shown separately from base rate** — 6C must be populated; cannot be embedded in Column 6A
12. **Missing "Final Payroll" checkbox** — failing to mark the last submission as final

## 3. PREVAILING WAGE RATE DETERMINATION

### How Are Rates Determined?

The DOL's Wage and Hour Division (WHD) conducts voluntary wage surveys among contractors in each geographic area. From this data, prevailing wages are determined using a **three-step methodology** (reinstated by the 2023 Final Rule, effective October 2023, reverting to pre-1982 standards):

| Step | Rule | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| **Step 1: Majority Rule** | If more than 50% of workers in a classification receive the same rate | That rate is the prevailing wage |
| **Step 2: 30% Rule** | If no majority, but at least 30% receive the same rate | That rate is the prevailing wage |
| **Step 3: Weighted Average** | If neither majority nor 30% threshold met | Total wages paid ÷ total workers surveyed |

**Example (Majority Rule):**
- 45 carpenters paid $23.50/hr (55% of 82 total) → Prevailing rate = $23.50

**Example (Weighted Average):**
- 25 @ $23.50, 15 @ $22.00, 10 @ $18.35, 7 @ $20.00, 5 @ $20.80 (62 total)
- Total wages = $1,345.00 → $1,345 ÷ 62 = **$21.69 prevailing rate**

The 2023 rule change is expected to result in **higher prevailing wage rates** in most areas, as the 30% rule often captures union scale rates even when unions represent less than 12% of construction workers nationally.

**Additional rate update mechanism (new in 2023):** DOL now uses the Employment Cost Index (ECI) for Construction to escalate non-CBA rates every three years between surveys, preventing rates from becoming stale.

### Where to Look Up Wage Rates

**Primary source — SAM.gov:**  
[https://sam.gov/wage-determinations](https://sam.gov/wage-determinations)

As of June 14, 2019, SAM.gov is the **official and only** source for Davis-Bacon General Wage Determinations (GWDs). It is free.

**How to search:**
1. Navigate to sam.gov → Wage Determinations
2. Select "Public Building or Works" filter for DBA construction
3. Filter by State, County, DBA Construction Type (Building/Highway/Heavy/Residential), and Publish Date
4. Search by partial WD number (e.g., "IL2021" for Illinois 2021 determinations)
5. View history of modifications for each WD

**Alternative for state rates:** Each state agency publishes its own rates. California DIR at dir.ca.gov, New York Department of Labor, etc.

---

### What Is a "Wage Determination"?

A **wage determination (WD)** is a formal listing of minimum prevailing wages (base hourly rate) and fringe benefit rates for each classification of laborers and mechanics in a specific geographic area and construction type, as determined by the WHD.

**WD number structure:** Three components:
1. State abbreviation + year (e.g., "NC2024")
2. Sequence number (e.g., "0005")
3. Revision number (e.g., "/2" for second revision)
Full example: NC20240005/2

**Two types of wage determinations:**

| Type | When Used | Validity |
|---|---|---|
| **General Wage Determination (GWD)** | Standard — by county and construction type, for all projects in that area | Continuous, with periodic modifications. Lock-in at bid date (new modifications incorporated up to bid opening; 90-day rule after bid opening) |
| **Project Wage Determination (PWD)** | Special cases: multi-county projects, no GWD for a county/type, or nearly all work is in a missing classification | Valid for **180 days** from issuance; requested via SF-308 form to WHD Branch of Construction Wage Determinations (DBA308project@dol.gov) |

**What's inside a wage determination:**
- All applicable construction types for the area
- Each classification name
- Basic hourly rate
- Fringe benefit rate
- Notes on specific scope of work (e.g., "LINEMAN - Construction of transmission lines 69 KV and above")
- Special notations for union-derived rates vs. survey rates

**Rate lock-in rules:**
- For new contracts: Rate in solicitation is locked at bid opening (with 90-day rule for modifications)
- For multi-year contracts: Contracting agency must incorporate current WD on each anniversary date of contract award

---

### Rate Structure: Base Rate + Fringe Benefits

**Total prevailing wage = Basic Hourly Rate (BHR) + Fringe Benefit Rate**

Example from DOL guidance:
```
Basic Hourly Rate:  $34.00
Fringe Benefits:    $21.00
Total Prevailing:   $55.00
```

A contractor may satisfy the $55.00 obligation in multiple ways:
- $55.00 in cash wages
- $34.00 cash + $21.00 in bona fide fringe contributions
- $32.00 cash + $23.00 in bona fide fringe contributions (excess cash wages above BHR offset fringe requirement)

**What counts as bona fide fringe benefits:**
- Health insurance (medical, dental, vision)
- Pension/retirement plan contributions
- Life insurance
- Disability insurance
- Vacation and holiday pay (if contributed to a bona fide fund, not just paid as cash)
- Apprenticeship training fund contributions
- Other employer contributions to approved trust funds

**What does NOT count:**
- Workers' compensation insurance (required by state law)
- FICA contributions (required by federal law)
- State unemployment taxes
- Any amount required by law

**Key annualization rule:** Fringe credits must be annualized — divided across ALL hours worked (both DBA and non-DBA), not just hours on covered projects. This prevents contractors from "double counting" fringe benefits provided for non-federal work.

---

### Common Trade Classifications

DBA wage determinations span four construction categories, each with distinct classification systems:

**BUILDING:**
- Carpenters (multiple sub-categories: rough, finish, millwork, formwork)
- Electricians (inside wireman, high voltage, telecommunications)
- Plumbers / Pipefitters / Steamfitters
- Iron Workers (structural, ornamental, reinforcing)
- Operating Engineers (by equipment type and capacity)
- Laborers (multiple sub-categories: concrete, demolition, hazardous materials)
- Painters (brush and roller vs. spray)
- Sheet Metal Workers (HVAC)
- Masons / Bricklayers / Plasterers / Tapers
- Elevator Constructors
- Glaziers
- Roofers
- Truck Drivers

**HIGHWAY:**
- Laborers (multiple sub-categories)
- Operating Engineers
- Truck Drivers (by truck type and capacity)
- Equipment operators specific to paving, grading, etc.

**HEAVY (dams, tunnels, power plants):**
- Ironworkers
- Operating Engineers (crane operators, heavy lift)
- Demolition workers

**RESIDENTIAL:**
- Carpenters
- Electricians
- Plumbers
- HVAC technicians
- Roofers

**CRITICAL:** There is **no universal classification list**. Classifications come exclusively from the project's applicable wage determination. A contractor must look at the specific WD for the project's county, state, and construction type.

---

### Handling Workers Who Perform Multiple Trades

**Rule:** Pay based on work **actually performed**, not job title.

- Worker must be classified based on the actual duties performed each day
- If a worker performs two different classifications in a single week, they must appear on **separate rows** on the WH-347 — one for each classification with hours broken out
- If the contractor **did not maintain an accurate breakdown** of hours by classification, they must pay ALL hours at the **highest applicable prevailing wage rate** for any classification performed

**Practical implication for software:** Time tracking must capture classification at the task/trade level, not just hours per day per project.

---

### What Happens When Rates Change Mid-Project?

- **General Wage Determinations:** Subject to periodic modifications. Once a project contract is awarded, the rate is generally **locked** at bid/award unless the contract is multi-year
- **Multi-year contracts:** On each anniversary date of contract award, the contracting agency must incorporate the current (most recent) WD revision
- **If wrong WD included in solicitation:** Agency must either (a) terminate and re-solicit with correct WD, or (b) incorporate correct WD retroactively to contract award, with contract price adjustment if appropriate
- **Practice:** Contractors should verify the WD revision incorporated in their specific contract — do not assume the most current SAM.gov version applies if the project pre-dates it

## 4. CERTIFIED PAYROLL REPORTING WORKFLOW

### Step-by-Step: How a GC Manages Certified Payroll

**Pre-Project Setup:**
1. Obtain the contract documents — wage determination numbers and revision numbers are embedded in the contract/solicitation
2. Look up all applicable WDs on SAM.gov
3. Set up wage determination tables in payroll system (classifications → rates → fringe amounts)
4. Register project in compliance platform (e.g., LCPtracker) — enter WD numbers, project details
5. Require all subcontractors to register in the compliance platform before mobilizing
6. Issue subcontract language that explicitly passes Davis-Bacon obligations and requires weekly CPR submission

**Weekly Cycle:**

| Step | Actor | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Field/timekeeping | Capture hours by worker, by classification, by project, by day — ST and OT separately |
| 2 | Payroll processor | Run payroll — ensure each worker's base + fringe meets prevailing rate for classification and hours |
| 3 | Payroll processor | Generate WH-347 or equivalent CPR |
| 4 | Certifying official | Review and sign Statement of Compliance (with valid e-signature) |
| 5 | Sub | Upload signed CPR to compliance platform or email to GC by deadline |
| 6 | GC | Review and approve/reject sub CPRs in compliance platform (70+ validation checks run automatically in LCPtracker) |
| 7 | GC | Submit own CPR + all approved sub CPRs to contracting agency |
| 8 | Agency | Reviews and archives; triggers investigation if issues found |

**Payment hold leverage:** Most federal contracts allow the GC to withhold payment from subs until compliant CPRs are received for the corresponding pay period.

---

### GC vs. Sub Responsibilities and Certification

| Responsibility | Prime Contractor | Subcontractor |
|---|---|---|
| Certify OWN payroll | Yes | Yes |
| Certify SUB payrolls | No — subs certify their own | N/A |
| Collect sub CPRs | Yes — responsible for collecting all subs' submissions | Must submit to GC |
| Submit to agency | Yes — responsible for ALL CPRs (own + subs) | Submit to GC; GC transmits to agency |
| Liability for sub violations | Yes — GC can be held liable for back wages regardless of intent | Liable for own violations |
| Subcontract flow-down | Must include Davis-Bacon clauses in all subcontracts | Must include in their sub-subcontracts |

**Key regulatory citation:** 29 CFR 5.5(a)(3)(ii)(A) — Prime contractors are "responsible for the submission of all certified payrolls by all subcontractors."

**Post-2023 rule change:** Prime contractors are now **strictly liable** for subcontractor back wages — the prior standard of showing lack of intent no longer applies as a complete defense.

---

### How Apprentice Rates Work

Apprentices may be paid **less than the journeyworker prevailing wage** for their classification, but ONLY if:

1. The apprentice is **individually registered** in an apprenticeship program approved by DOL's Office of Apprenticeship (OA) or a recognized State Apprenticeship Agency (SAA)
2. The apprentice wage is set by the approved apprenticeship program's wage progression schedule
3. The apprentice-to-journeyworker **ratio** specified in the program is maintained on the project
4. If the ratio is exceeded, the excess apprentices must be paid the full journeyworker prevailing wage

**Reporting:** Column 2 of WH-347 must show "RA" for registered apprentices, with their progression level. Box 4 of the Statement of Compliance must be completed with the apprenticeship program name, registering body (OA or SAA), and classification.

**Fringe benefits for apprentices:** Must be paid in accordance with the apprenticeship program's provisions. If the program is silent on fringes, apprentices receive the full fringe amount listed on the wage determination for the journeyworker classification.

**Apprenticeship program cost credits:** Contractors may claim a fringe credit for DOL-approved apprenticeship program costs (instruction, books, tools) — annualized across all hours worked in that classification.

---

### Fringe Benefit Payment Methods

**Option A — Bona fide fringe benefit plans:**
- Irrevocable contributions to a trustee or third-party fund
- Must be a "bona fide" plan (established plan, not merely promised benefits)
- Examples: union trust funds, health insurance plans, 401(k), pension funds, vacation funds

**Option B — Cash in lieu of fringe benefits:**
- Pay the fringe benefit amount as additional cash wages
- Simple to administer, but **increases taxable wages** (subject to FICA, withholding, etc.)
- Still must track and report separately — Column 6C on WH-347

**Option C — Combination:**
- Most common in non-union shops — some existing benefits (health insurance) satisfy part of the fringe requirement; remainder paid as cash in lieu

**Annualization of fringe credits:**
When a contractor contributes to fringe plans covering both DBA and non-DBA workers, the hourly credit per DBA worker is calculated by spreading annual plan costs across ALL hours worked during the year (not just DBA hours). This prevents the contractor from using fringes paid on private work to satisfy DBA obligations.

**Formula:**
```
Hourly Fringe Credit = Total Annual Plan Cost ÷ Total Hours Worked by All Workers in That Classification
```

### Records That Must Be Maintained

Under 29 CFR 5.5(a)(3)(i)(A) and the 2023 Final Rule, contractors must maintain:

| Record Category | Specific Elements |
|---|---|
| **Basic payroll records** | Name, SSN, **last known address**, **telephone number**, **email address** (new in 2023 rule) |
| **Work classification records** | Classification(s) of work actually performed (not just job title) |
| **Compensation records** | Hourly rates paid; rates of fringe contributions or cash equivalents; type of each fringe benefit |
| **Hours records** | Daily and weekly hours actually worked in total AND on each covered contract |
| **Deductions** | All deductions made from wages |
| **Payment records** | Actual wages paid to each worker |
| **Certified payrolls** | Copy of every WH-347 (or equivalent) submitted |
| **Statements of Compliance** | Signed SOC accompanying each CPR |
| **Contracts/subcontracts** | Copy of prime contract and all subcontracts, including bids, proposals, amendments, modifications, and extensions |
| **Wage determination** | Copy of applicable WD incorporated in contract |

**Retention period:** **3 years after completion of all work on the prime contract**

- If the prime contract runs 5 years, a sub that finished in year 1 must keep records until year 8 (5 year project + 3 years after prime contract completion)
- If litigation, claim, or audit is initiated before 3 years, records must be kept until resolution + final action

**Record availability:** Must be available for inspection by WHD and contracting agency. Failure to produce records upon request can result in:
- Suspension of payments, advances, or guarantees
- Preclusion from using those records in administrative proceedings (DOL may take the worker's allegations at face value)

### What Triggers a DOL Investigation?

**Direct triggers:**
1. **Worker complaint** — most common. A single complaint to the WHD is sufficient to open an investigation. Workers may complain about classification, pay rates, overtime, or missing fringe benefits
2. **Missing or late CPR submissions** — agencies track submissions; gaps trigger inquiry
3. **CPR discrepancies** — hours on certified payroll don't match daily logs; fringe credits don't match stated plan amounts
4. **Contractor competitor tips** — competitors who believe an underbidding contractor is non-compliant may report
5. **Media reports** — public coverage of labor disputes or wage theft allegations

**Systematic/program triggers:**
6. **DOL directed investigations** — WHD selects employers in high-violation industries/geographies without requiring a complaint. Construction is considered high-risk
7. **Multi-agency referrals** — OSHA investigations, IRS tax audits, state unemployment claims, or I-9 audits may surface wage violations and be referred to WHD
8. **Audit frequency escalation** — contractors with prior violations face increased audit frequency (3–5 year enhanced monitoring period)

**Investigation process:**
1. Preliminary review of certified payrolls submitted
2. Request for internal "in-house" payroll records, timecards, and benefit plan documentation
3. Worker interviews (typically conducted privately on-site; contractor may not interfere)
4. Examination of fringe benefit plan documents and contribution records
5. Conformance determination if classifications are disputed
6. Final conference with contractor
7. Summary of Unpaid Wages issued; contractor given opportunity to pay or dispute

### Can WH-347 Be E-Filed?

**Direct federal agency submission:** No centralized federal e-filing portal exists for WH-347. Submission is typically:
- Email PDF to contracting agency
- Upload to agency-specific portal or FAPIIS
- Submit through compliance platforms (LCPtracker, eMars) where the agency has accounts

**Electronically managed platforms:** LCPtracker, B2Gnow, and Elation function as intermediary platforms where CPRs are submitted electronically within the system and made accessible to agencies via the platform's dashboard.

**DOL's new fillable form** (December 2025): The DOL released an online fillable WH-347 that generates a print-ready PDF. Data never leaves the user's computer — not a submission portal, just a form generator.

**State e-filing portals (mandatory in some states):**
- **California:** Must submit to DIR's eCPR online system (XML format)
- **New York:** Mandatory submission through NYSDOL's Certified Payroll system starting January 1, 2026
- **Washington:** WA L&I XML format required

### E-Signatures on Statement of Compliance

**Legally valid:** Electronic signatures are fully accepted on the Statement of Compliance, per DOL guidance dating to 2004 and codified in the 2023 Final Rule. Authority: Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN), Government Paperwork Elimination Act (GPEA).

**Requirements for valid e-signature:**
1. An electronic process that indicates acceptance of the certified payroll record
2. Includes an electronic method of verifying the signer's identity
3. NOT a photocopy or scan of a handwritten signature

**How platforms implement this:** LCPtracker requires users to create a separate e-signature PIN/password from their login credentials. When certifying a payroll, the user enters their e-signature credential — this constitutes the legally valid electronic acceptance.

### Biggest Pain Points Contractors Face

1. **Worker misclassification risk** — The classification must match the WD, not the contractor's internal job title. On a project with 10 trades and a WD with 40+ classifications, this is a high-error-rate manual task.

2. **Multi-classification time tracking** — Capturing which hours a worker spent in which classification requires trade-level timecard detail. Most payroll systems capture by project code only, not by trade classification per day.

3. **Subcontractor CPR collection** — GCs are responsible for sub compliance but have no authority over sub payroll systems. Getting weekly CPRs on time from 20+ subs is a significant administrative burden. Missing subs can delay GC payment from the agency.

4. **Fringe benefit calculation complexity** — Annualization rules, partial benefit satisfaction, tracking multiple benefit plans per worker, computing cash-in-lieu amounts accurately — all manual and error-prone without specialized software.

5. **State vs. federal overlap** — On a joint-funded project, the contractor must determine whether state or federal rate is higher and pay the higher. With different classification systems, this comparison is non-trivial.

6. **Wage determination currency** — WDs get modified. Contractors may not know their WD was revised until they receive a compliance notice from the agency. Rate changes mid-project require retroactive payroll corrections.

7. **Weekly submission burden** — The DOL estimates 55 minutes per WH-347 form. A GC with 5 subs on a 40-week project submits or oversees 200+ CPR submissions — ~183 hours of administrative work per project.

8. **Manual WH-347 completion** — The revised 2024 WH-347 has more fields than the prior version. Manual completion in PDF or Excel is tedious and error-prone. Many contractors still fill forms by hand or use basic Excel templates.

9. **Audit preparedness** — When a DOL investigator arrives, contractors need instant access to 3 years of records organized by project, contractor, and week. Paper files or disorganized digital archives create massive exposure.

10. **Apprentice ratio management** — Contractors who want to use apprentice rates must maintain approved ratios in real-time. Without automated tracking, it's easy to exceed ratios and trigger journeyworker rate requirements retroactively.

### What Would Make a Construction PM Tool's Certified Payroll Feature a "Killer" Feature

**vs. Standalone Compliance Platforms (LCPtracker, Elation):**

A construction PM platform has access to data that standalone compliance tools don't — project schedules, daily reports, field time capture, subcontractor management. The killer feature is **eliminating the disconnect between PM data and payroll compliance**:

1. **Classification-aware time capture** — If workers are logging time in the PM field app, add a trade classification field. Certified payroll becomes nearly auto-generated from timecards.

2. **Sub compliance gating on payments** — Payment application workflows already exist in PM tools. Gate pay apps on certified payroll compliance status. This eliminates the GC's need to chase subs for CPRs.

3. **Daily log vs. CPR cross-check** — Daily field reports capture who was on-site. Automatically compare against the CPR submitted for that week. Flag discrepancies before they become violations.

4. **Wage determination import + alert** — Pull WD from SAM.gov at project setup. Alert PM when WD revision is published during project. Tie the alert directly to the subcontract/procurement workflow.

5. **Single source of truth for workers** — PM tools have sub rosters and worker lists. Pre-populating CPR with known workers eliminates redundant data entry.

6. **Audit-ready document package** — One-click export of all CPRs + WDs + contract documents for DOL audit response.

**Positioning:** Standalone compliance tools require a separate login, separate data entry, and create siloed compliance workflows. A PM-embedded feature means compliance becomes a natural byproduct of normal project management work rather than a separate administrative burden.

### Simultaneous Federal and State Applicability

On many projects, **both federal and state prevailing wage laws apply simultaneously**. This occurs when:
- A project receives **both federal and state funding** (very common for transportation, water/sewer, public housing)
- State law independently covers the project AND federal funding flows to it

**Resolution:** Contractors must compare the federal WD rates and the state prevailing wage rates for each classification, and **pay whichever is higher** for each classification.

This is complex because:
- Federal and state classification systems may not align directly (different names for equivalent trades)
- State rates may be higher in some classifications and lower in others
- State may require different reporting forms in addition to or instead of WH-347

**Software must handle:**
- Import of both federal WD and state wage determination for the same project
- Classification mapping between federal and state systems
- Rate comparison logic with "pay higher" enforcement
- Generation of both WH-347 (or equivalent) AND state-specific CPR form

### 2023 Final Rule Summary

The DOL's August 2023 Final Rule (effective October 23, 2023) made the first major overhaul of Davis-Bacon regulations in 40+ years:

1. **30% rule reinstated** — prevailing wage is the rate paid to at least 30% of workers (not just majority)
2. **ECI escalation** — non-CBA rates updated every 3 years using Employment Cost Index
3. **Urban/rural combination** — returned to pre-1982 practice of combining urban and rural rates in surveys
4. **State rate adoption** — DOL can incorporate certain state prevailing wage rates into federal determinations
5. **Expanded DBRA coverage** — attempted to expand coverage to material suppliers and certain delivery drivers (three provisions enjoined by Texas federal court in June 2024)
6. **Prime contractor strict liability** — primes responsible for sub back wages without showing intent
7. **Cross-contract withholding** — DOL can withhold across contractor's other federal contracts
8. **Enhanced recordkeeping** — added email addresses and phone numbers to required records; explicit 3-year retention
9. **Electronic systems** — explicitly authorized for CPR submission and retention
10. **Anti-retaliation provisions** — strengthened worker protections

**Litigation note:** Three provisions were nationally enjoined (June 2024, N.D. Texas): (1) expanded "laborers or mechanics" definition covering material suppliers; (2) coverage of delivery truck drivers for on-site time; (3) operation-of-law provision making DBRA apply even if contracting agency omitted it from the contract.
